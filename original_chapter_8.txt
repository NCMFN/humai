ABSTRACT
The convergence of sixth-generation (6G) wireless networks, the Internet of Medical Things (IoMT), and artificial intelligence (AI) is reshaping healthcare delivery from a reactive discipline into a continuous, data-driven practice. With projected terabit-per-second throughput, sub-microsecond latency, and native AI integration, 6G will underpin a new generation of wearable biosensors, remote diagnostics, personalised treatment systems, and immersive telemedicine platforms. This chapter examines the technical foundations that make this transformation possible, the layered architecture required to implement it, and the security, ethical, and policy dimensions that will determine whether its benefits are equitably realised. Key topics include the 6G-IoMT integration framework, edge-cloud coordination, remote patient monitoring case studies, explainable AI governance, and federated learning as a privacy-preserving research tool. The chapter concludes that successful deployment requires not only technical innovation but sustained cross-disciplinary collaboration, rigorous regulatory frameworks, and deliberate design choices that embed security and fairness from the outset.
Keywords: 6G networks; Internet of Medical Things (IoMT); wearable devices; remote patient monitoring; edge computing; explainable AI; federated learning; digital medical twins; telemedicine; healthcare security.

1. INTRODUCTION
Healthcare systems worldwide are undergoing a structural transformation, driven by the simultaneous maturation of artificial intelligence, sensor miniaturisation, and next-generation wireless infrastructure. The transition from fifth-generation (5G) to sixth-generation (6G) wireless networks is not, in this context, a purely technical upgrade, it is the enabling condition for a fundamentally different model of care. Whereas previous mobile generations expanded what clinicians could do within hospital walls, 6G extends that capability across geography, time, and patient populations.
At the centre of this shift stands the Internet of Medical Things (IoMT); a rapidly growing ecosystem of wearable biosensors, implantable monitors, and smart clinical devices that generate continuous physiological data. When those data streams are transmitted over 6G networks and processed by AI-driven analytics pipelines, the clinical possibilities are substantial. Chronic conditions can be managed between appointments rather than sampled at them. Deterioration can be flagged before it becomes acute. Personalised treatment protocols can be updated in real time as patient data evolves. Remote surgery, once a speculative concept, moves within reach of practical deployment.
This chapter is structured to examine these possibilities systematically while attending to the complications they introduce. It is intended for readers across computer science, clinical informatics, communications engineering, and health policy who wish to understand both the technical foundations and the broader implications of 6G-IoMT integration. The argument developed here is that the transformation in question is technically feasible but contingent on institutional decisions; about security architecture, regulatory frameworks, ethical governance, and equitable access, that must be made well before 6G reaches commercial scale.
The chapter is organised as follows. Section 2 establishes the technical foundations of 6G. Section 3 surveys the wearable and IoMT device landscape. Section 4 details integration frameworks and data architectures. Section 5 presents diagnostic use cases through case studies. Section 6 addresses security, privacy, and ethics. Section 7 maps the most important research directions and policy challenges. Section 8 draws conclusions.








2. TECHNOLOGICAL FOUNDATIONS: 6G AND SMART HEALTHCARE
To understand what 6G makes possible in healthcare, it is necessary first to understand what distinguishes it technically from its predecessor. The differences are not merely quantitative; faster speeds, lower delays but architectural: 6G represents a different philosophy of network design.
2.1 6G vs. 5G: Technical Evolution
Fifth-generation networks delivered meaningful improvements over 4G LTE, primarily in mobile broadband speed and machine-to-machine communication. Yet the demands of a truly instrumented healthcare system, one in which millions of devices transmit continuously, and in which clinical decisions are made on the basis of those transmissions , exceed what 5G was designed to accommodate.
Sixth-generation wireless technology addresses this gap through three primary advances.
1.   Data Rate: While 5G operates in the gigabit-per-second range, 6G targets throughput of 1 terabit per second (VDE, 2024). For healthcare, this supports frictionless transfer of large imaging datasets, full genomic sequences, and high-resolution video simultaneously across a single connection.
2.   Latency: 6G compresses end-to-end communication delays from the millisecond range of 5G to sub-microsecond levels. This is not a marginal improvement, it is the difference between a remote surgical system that feels instantaneous and one in which latency is perceptible and potentially dangerous.
3.   Device Connectivity: Where 5G supports approximately 1 million connected devices per square kilometre, 6G targets 10 million. This enables truly pervasive IoMT deployment, every patient room, every wearable device, every environmental sensor, connected simultaneously (A. K. S. A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2024).

Beyond these quantitative leaps, 6G introduces architectural innovations that were nascent or absent in 5G. Terahertz (THz) communication exploits frequency bands well above current commercial licences, unlocking bandwidth previously unavailable for mobile systems. Massive Multiple Input Multiple Output (MIMO) antenna configurations push spectral efficiency toward theoretical limits. Integrated Sensing and Communication (ISAC) allows a single network layer to perform both data exchange and environmental sensing simultaneously (How AI-Driven Diagnostics Are Transforming Healthcare Delivery–DelveInsight, 2025). Together, these define 6G as a qualitative departure, not merely a quantitative extension.
2.2 Capabilities Relevant to Healthcare (Latency, Bandwidth, Edge Computing)
The performance parameters that distinguish 6G translate into concrete clinical dividends across three primary dimensions.
1. Ultra-Low Latency
In most everyday applications, a delay of a few milliseconds passes unnoticed. In healthcare, however, latency is not merely a quality-of-experience metric, it can be the difference between a successful remote surgical procedure and a catastrophic error. Sixth-generation networks target end-to-end delays in the range of single-digit microseconds, a specification that enables the closed-loop, bidirectional control required for robotic-assisted telesurgery and real-time vital sign monitoring with embedded alert logic. Where any perceptible lag in command transmission could compromise instrument positioning or delay a life-saving notification, this capability transitions from a technical advantage to a clinical necessity.
2. Massive Bandwidth and Throughput
The clinical utility of remote diagnostics has historically been constrained by available data bandwidth. High-resolution medical imaging; including computed tomography, magnetic resonance imaging, and whole-slide pathology digitisation, generates file sizes that most current networks transmit with noticeable delay. 6G’s terabit-per-second throughput removes that constraint, enabling frictionless exchange of complex patient data between geographically dispersed care teams. Extended reality modalities for surgical planning or neurological rehabilitation become clinically viable in this bandwidth environment (Rajab, 2024).
3. Edge Computing
The edge computing paradigm, natively integrated into 6G architecture, reorients data processing away from centralised cloud infrastructure and toward the physical location where data originates. For healthcare, this means computationally intensive analytics can be performed within the hospital building, or even at the bedside, rather than routed through remote data centres. Beyond latency reduction, a property that is perhaps more significant for clinical adoption is that sensitive patient data need not leave the immediate care environment for preliminary processing, substantially reducing exposure to interception risk.
2.3 How 6G Supports Intelligent Healthcare
The contribution of 6G to healthcare extends beyond improved communication parameters. When a high-bandwidth, low-latency network is coupled with AI, IoT, blockchain, and digital twin technologies, the combined infrastructure supports a fundamentally different temporal orientation in care delivery: rather than intervening after pathology has declared itself, the system gathers continuous data, identifies deviating trajectories, and prompts intervention before thresholds are crossed.
A compelling illustration of this shift is the emerging concept of the Digital Medical Twin. Creating a high-fidelity virtual replica of a patient; continuously updated from wearable sensor streams, electronic health records, and imaging data, depends precisely on the ultra-reliable, low-latency communication fabric that 6G promises to deliver (Rajab, 2024). Such a model can be subjected to simulated interventions without any risk to the actual patient. Equipment maintenance schedules can be optimised through predictive failure modelling. The result is a healthcare ecosystem capable of anticipating needs and reconfiguring care pathways dynamically.
Case Example: Smart Hospital Operations In a 6G-enabled hospital, consider the following simultaneous operations:
1.   Patient Monitoring: Wearable sensors on hundreds of patients transmit vital signs every few seconds. AI algorithms flag anomalies in real time, alerting nursing staff before clinical deterioration is visually apparent.
2.   Surgical Suite: A remote specialist guides a local surgical team via a haptic-feedback robotic system over a 6G link, with communication delay imperceptible to both parties.
3.  Pharmacy and Equipment: IoT-enabled medication dispensers and equipment trackers coordinate autonomously, reducing waste and preventing stock-outs.
4.   Administrative Systems: Digital twin models of ward occupancy inform bed management decisions in real time, optimising patient flow across the facility.
The interdependencies this creates also introduce new risk, a single compromised node could cascade across multiple clinical systems. This reality makes security architecture not an operational afterthought but a foundational design constraint.



3. WEARABLE DEVICES AND IOMT IN HEALTHCARE
3.1 Types and Functions of Wearables
The IoMT encompasses a heterogeneous collection of interconnected devices, ranging from off-the-shelf consumer fitness bands to sophisticated hospital-grade physiological monitors and surgically implanted biosensors (as illustrated in Figure 8.1).



Figure 8.1 Application of Wearable Devices in IoMT
 This spectrum includes the following major categories.
1.   Consumer Wearables: Smartwatches, fitness trackers, and biosensor patches monitor heart rate, sleep quality, activity levels, and peripheral oxygen saturation. While designed for general wellness, many now carry clinical-grade accuracy for specific metrics.
2. Medical-Grade Wearables: Continuous glucose monitors (CGMs), ambulatory electrocardiogram patches, and blood pressure cuffs designed for clinical deployment offer the precision required for therapeutic decision-making.
3.  Implantable Sensors: Cardiac defibrillators, cochlear implants, and glucose-sensing microneedles provide direct physiological access that surface-worn devices cannot achieve. These generate continuous data on metabolic and electrophysiological parameters critical for chronic disease management.
4.   Smart Clinical Equipment: Hospital beds embedded with pressure and movement sensors, smart infusion pumps, and ventilators with real-time analytics capabilities represent the clinical infrastructure layer of IoMT.
What unifies these device categories is their shared function: automated, continuous collection of physiological parameters. Heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, body temperature, glucose concentration, respiratory rate, physical activity, and inferred affective states all fall within the current sensing repertoire (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c). The data they yield constitutes the raw material from which AI-driven monitoring and diagnostic systems extract clinically actionable insights.
3.2 Monitoring, Real-time Feedback, and Data Analytics
The clinical promise of IoMT rests on its capacity to sustain unbroken physiological surveillance rather than sampling a patient’s state at discrete appointments. Continuous monitoring opens a temporal window on health that episodic assessment cannot provide. Subtle trends, a slowly drifting resting heart rate, gradual changes in gait symmetry, incremental narrowing of pulse pressure, become detectable only when data is collected at sufficient frequency over sufficient time (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c).
Artificial intelligence brings several distinct capabilities to the analysis of these data streams.
1.   Predictive Diagnostics: Algorithms trained on large IoMT datasets can identify physiological signals of impending deterioration; early sepsis indicators, arrhythmia precursors, hypoglycaemic episode patterns, before they become clinically manifest. The operational value is significant: earlier intervention is almost universally associated with better outcomes and lower treatment cost.
2.   Personalised Treatment Recommendations: AI outcome models draw on individual patient trajectories, not merely population averages, to propose interventions that account for idiosyncratic physiology. In physiotherapy and rehabilitation, real-time protocol adaptation guided by AI offers more efficient recovery pathways than fixed-schedule approaches.
3.   Real-time Decision Support: Evidence-distilled summaries presented to clinicians at the point of care allow rapid, informed decisions without manual interrogation of large data archives (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c).
4.   Multi-modal Analytics: Beyond vital signs, AI platforms analyse medical images, parse genomic data for disease risk stratification, and infer mental health indicators from behavioural sensor streams creating a multi-dimensional picture of patient health (A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2023).

The benefits of IoMT integration are both clinical and systemic. Patients with stable chronic conditions can be assessed remotely without the cost and inconvenience of travel. Clinicians gain access to richer longitudinal data than any clinic visit could provide. Early detection of physiological anomalies allows intervention before conditions become acute. Patients in geographically marginalised communities, or those with mobility constraints, benefit disproportionately: barriers that previously made specialist access prohibitive are substantially lowered when monitoring can occur at home (S. Technical limitations, including battery longevity constraints and the complexity of integrating heterogeneous devices into existing hospital IT architectures, further slow deployment.














4. INTEGRATION MODELS AND IMPLEMENTATION FRAMEWORKS
4.1 Proposed Architecture for 6G-IoMT Integration
The architecture required to integrate IoMT devices with 6G infrastructure is best understood as a layered hierarchy, each stratum performing a discrete set of functions while remaining interoperable with the strata above and below it.
The five layers of the architecture function as follows:
Device Layer: This is the physical sensing foundation consisting of implantable sensors, IoT medical devices, and wearable sensors. These devices continuously harvest physiological data, generating the raw streams that feed the entire system (A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2025b).
Communication Layer: This layer provides the backbone for 6G wireless connectivity, utilizing IoT protocols and secure transmission to move data from the device fleet without bottlenecks, ensuring the ultra-low latency required for clinical applications.
Edge / Fog Layer: Analytic labor begins here with real-time anomaly detection, local health data analytics, and edge AI processing. This layer handles latency-sensitive computations physically closer to the user to ensure immediate responsiveness.
Cloud Intelligence Layer: This layer provides massive storage and high-level processing power. It manages medical data storage, big data analytics, and the training of AI/ML models, while also maintaining digital health twins for long-term patient modeling.
Application Layer: Processed outputs are translated into functional clinical services, including predictive healthcare systems, telemedicine, clinical decision support, and remote patient monitoring (A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2025b).
Future iterations of this architecture will incorporate AI-native network layers that embed intelligence into routing and resource allocation decisions; multi-access edge computing (MEC) frameworks distributing processing across physically proximate nodes; ISAC capabilities enabling the network to simultaneously sense its environment and communicate within it; and extended reality (XR) interfaces for clinicians and patients (Rajab, 2024). The combined effect, illustrated in Figure 8.2, is a healthcare ecosystem considerably more responsive than anything 5G infrastructure supports.



4.2 Data Flow, Edge/Cloud Coordination, and Device Communication
Understanding how data moves through this architecture clarifies both its capabilities and its vulnerabilities (as illustrated in Figure 8.3).
Data Flow
Sensor devices at the perception layer emit continuous data streams covering physiological parameters, activity metrics, and environmental variables. These streams pass through encrypted communication links toward edge nodes for immediate processing, or toward cloud platforms for less time-sensitive analytics. A tiered approach ensures data is processed at the computational level best matched to its urgency: vital sign anomalies triggering emergency alerts are analysed locally at the edge within milliseconds, while long-term trend analysis and model retraining occur asynchronously in the cloud (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c).


Edge/Cloud Coordination
1.   Edge Computing: When AI inference runs on an edge node within the hospital perimeter, raw patient data need not traverse external network segments, a property that meaningfully reduces the attack surface for interception. Edge-deployed models can be trained and updated locally using federated techniques, limiting the centralisation of identifiable health records. This makes edge computing critical not only for performance but also for privacy (A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2025d).
2.   Cloud Computing: Cloud platforms retain a critical role by providing the storage capacity and computational depth required for functions infeasible at the edge: training large deep learning models, maintaining longitudinal patient records, supporting population health surveillance, and enabling cross-institutional research collaborations.

Device Communication Protocols
Current IoMT deployments rely on a patchwork of wireless protocols, 5G for latency-critical applications, Narrowband-IoT (NB-IoT) and LoRa for low-power wide-area monitoring in resource-constrained settings. The 6G transition will substantially rationalise this landscape by offering terahertz-band communication with sufficient bandwidth to accommodate protocol diversity within a single network layer (VDE, 2024). Novel healthcare-specific protocols are concurrently under development to address the data silo problem, the persistent tendency of proprietary device ecosystems to trap data in formats incompatible with broader health information architectures.
4.3 Implementation and Scalability Challenges
The gap between the architectural vision described above and large-scale clinical deployment is substantial. Several challenge categories deserve explicit acknowledgement.
1.   Infrastructure Upgrades: Realising 6G in healthcare settings requires installation of dense small-cell antenna networks and the upgrade or replacement of legacy medical devices and hospital IT platforms, a capital-intensive undertaking for which most health systems are not currently budgeted.
2.   High Costs: The aggregate cost of 6G infrastructure deployment, IoMT device procurement, system integration, and ongoing maintenance constitutes a substantial financial barrier, particularly for healthcare providers in lower-income economies where clinical need may be greatest.
3.   Data Management Complexity: A fully instrumented 6G-IoMT hospital generates data at volumes that challenge current storage architectures and processing pipelines. The heterogeneity of the data; structured sensor readings, clinical notes, and large imaging files  adds computational complexity to already demanding real-time processing requirements.
4.   Interoperability and Standardisation: The absence of universal communication standards across device manufacturers and health IT vendors creates ecosystem fragmentation. Data captured by one device frequently cannot be ingested by another vendor’s analytics platform without costly custom integration work (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c).
5.   Scalability: As device counts expand and patient populations grow, the integrated system must scale without degrading performance, security, or reliability. Network architectures that perform adequately with thousands of connected devices may require fundamental redesign to maintain those properties with millions (How AI-Driven Diagnostics Are Transforming Healthcare Deliver–DelveInsight, 2025).




5. CASE STUDIES AND USE CASES IN DIAGNOSTICS
The integration of 6G, IoMT, and AI does not remain at the level of architectural abstraction; it manifests in concrete use cases grounded in verifiable technical evidence and active pilot programmes.
5.1 Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM)
AI-driven Remote Patient Monitoring enables continuous oversight of chronic and post-acute conditions by analysing real-time wearable data to generate personalised alerts and recommendations (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c). Three case studies illustrate both the clinical potential and the practical mechanics of these systems.
Case Study 1: Diabetes Management
A study evaluating AI-driven RPM for diabetes management paired continuous glucose monitoring sensors with a machine learning engine trained to interpret glycaemic patterns and generate personalized dietary, medication, and alert recommendations. Over a six-month observation period, enrolled participants achieved a 20% reduction in HbA1c levels, a clinically meaningful improvement in long-term blood glucose control. Adherence to therapeutic protocols improved substantially, a finding attributed to the immediacy and personalisation of feedback, which contrasted with the delayed and generalised guidance available through traditional care pathways.
Case Study 2: Post-Surgical Monitoring
Following cardiac surgery, a second RPM programme deployed continuous monitoring of vital signs;  heart rate, arterial blood pressure, peripheral oxygen saturation, and ambulatory activity, with automated alerts triggered by deviations from individually calibrated baseline ranges. The system’s ability to flag early indicators of post-operative complications, including arrhythmias, wound infections, and haemodynamic instability, contributed to a 30% reduction in hospital readmissions. The clinical and economic implications are substantial: readmissions following cardiac surgery carry high cost, elevated mortality risk, and considerable patient burden.
Case Study 3: Elderly Care Monitoring
A third programme applied AI-powered RPM to an elderly care population, combining vital sign monitoring with fall detection and functional decline indicators. Continuous surveillance enabled clinicians to intervene before minor physiological deviations escalated into emergency presentations, improving outcomes for a cohort particularly vulnerable to the consequences of delayed care.
The progression illustrated by these case studies, from passive observation to active, personalised intervention, reflects a broader conceptual shift made possible by 6G connectivity combined with AI analytics. Monitoring is no longer merely descriptive; it is predictive and interventional.
5.2 Individualized AI Treatments
High-throughput 6G connectivity enables AI algorithms to process imaging, genomic, and real-time sensor data concurrently in order to generate diagnostically meaningful inferences and guide therapeutic decision-making (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025b).
Key clinical applications include:
1.   Early Sepsis Prediction: Models have demonstrated the capacity to identify at-risk patients several hours before clinical signs become apparent to bedside staff, a window of opportunity that, when translated into earlier antibiotic administration, is associated with substantially improved survival rates.
2.   Oncology and Cardiovascular Risk Stratification: AI algorithms analyse high-resolution imaging and genomic data in real time, providing immediate diagnostic feedback and facilitating early detection of diseases such as cancer and cardiovascular disorders (A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2025c).
3.   Adaptive Rehabilitation Protocols: In physiotherapy, where treatment programmes have historically been refined through trial and error, AI-guided adaptation of exercise intensity and frequency in real time offers more efficient recovery trajectories and better adherence.
5.3 Telemedicine
The telemedicine use case for 6G is both the most intuitive and, in terms of geographic equity, perhaps the most consequential. Ultra-high-speed connectivity with minimal latency enables consultations of quality sufficient to support clinical examination, not merely conversation.
1.   Remote Surgery (Telesurgery): When a robotic surgical platform is interfaced with a 6G network, the latency between a surgeon’s control inputs and the robotic instrument’s movements can be reduced to a level imperceptible to the operating clinician. This enables geographically distant surgeons to perform or guide complex procedures at remote sites, extending specialist capability to hospitals that cannot recruit or retain surgical expertise locally (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Hagri, 2025). AI systems embedded in the robotic platform provide haptic feedback, anomaly warnings, and procedural guidance.
2.   Immersive Consultations: Extended reality communication platforms supported by 6G’s bandwidth capacity allow clinicians and patients to interact through spatially immersive environments. Holographic visualisation of anatomical structures during pre-operative counselling, and virtual reality for chronic pain management or neurological rehabilitation, represent therapeutic modalities previously logistically prohibitive for most healthcare settings (MedicalExpo, 2024).
The democratising implication of 6G-enabled telemedicine, its potential to extend specialist-grade care to underserved and geographically remote populations, deserves emphasis. Healthcare equity is not merely a desirable outcome; for large portions of the global population, it remains a fundamental unmet need.
5.4 Solutions to Sustainability, Data Burden, and Ethics
1.   Sustainability: 6G network design incorporates energy efficiency objectives that reflect the sector’s growing accountability to environmental targets, including Net Zero commitments (Kaur & Gupta, 2025a). Energy harvesting from ambient thermal, kinetic, and electromagnetic sources is under active development for low-power IoMT devices. Environmental intelligence algorithms that adapt transmission parameters to real-time channel conditions further mitigate the spectrum-energy trade-off inherent in dense device deployments.
2.   Data Burden: AI systems address the cognitive demands of large IoMT data volumes by performing first-pass analysis at scale, surfacing only patterns that warrant clinician attention. Blockchain-based data governance frameworks complement this by creating tamper-evident, auditable ledgers of data provenance and access history, supporting accountability without centralising sensitive information (H. K. S. A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2023).
3.   Ethics: The responsible deployment of AI in clinical settings requires governance frameworks that embed ethical principles at the design stage. Informed consent, algorithmic transparency, equitable access, and preservation of patient autonomy must be operationalised as design requirements rather than post-hoc compliance checkboxes (Lekadir et al., 2025).
5.5 Real-world Exemplars or Hypothetical Application Models
While full-scale 6G commercial rollout remains several years away, research prototypes and pilot programmes are already yielding evidence of clinical feasibility.
1.   Google’s Prenatal Monitoring Initiative: This programme integrates AI-driven sensing capabilities within a 6G-testbed framework to monitor foetal and maternal wellbeing, demonstrating that the real-time data fusion required for IoMT-based prenatal care is technically achievable at prototype scale.
2.   Guangzhou Physiotherapy System: A physiotherapy application developed within Guangzhou’s 6G health infrastructure permits real-time personalisation of treatment parameters for patients in dedicated physiotherapy facilities, providing early evidence that adaptive AI therapy can be integrated into actual clinical workflow.
These early demonstrations, while not yet generalisable to full clinical deployment, provide meaningful evidence that the architectural vision outlined in this chapter is grounded in engineering reality.










6. SECURITY, PRIVACY, AND ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS
The expanded connectivity surface inherent in 6G deployment does not simply amplify existing IoMT security risks; it introduces qualitatively new threat vectors that demand correspondingly novel defensive architectures.
6.1 Health Data Risks and Security Strategies
Each distinctive capability of 6G corresponds to a distinctive security exposure, as summarised in Table 8.4. The five primary threat categories are described below.
1.   Immersive Communication Vulnerabilities: Remote surgical and diagnostic sessions conducted over immersive communication channels carry heightened interception risk. Unlike a conventional video call, the interception of a telesurgery data stream could enable alteration of instrument control signals or corruption of vital-sign telemetry, potentially with lethal consequences (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025a). Protecting these channels requires end-to-end encryption with integrity verification at every transmission hop.
2.   HRLLC Attacks: The very feature that makes 6G indispensable for time-critical procedures,  its commitment to microsecond latency, creates an exploitable dependency. Denial of Service (DoS) campaigns or packet injection schemes can degrade communication quality below clinical viability at critical moments, potentially disrupting remote surgical procedures mid-operation (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025a).
3.   Massive Connectivity as Attack Surface: 6G’s capacity to sustain connections for billions of simultaneously active devices means that any under-secured device constitutes a potential network entry point. A compromised bedside monitor or smart infusion pump could serve as a pivot from which an attacker traverses the broader hospital network, accessing electronic health records, manipulating device configurations, or disrupting clinical operations (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025a).
4.   Ubiquitous Connectivity Spreading Breaches: When coverage spans hospital buildings, ambulances, home care environments, and rural monitoring stations within a single integrated network, a security compromise originating in one node can propagate laterally with exceptional speed (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025a). Network segmentation, anomaly detection, and rapid containment protocols become critical defensive capabilities.
5.   AI Model Vulnerabilities: The AI systems performing diagnostic inference are themselves attack surfaces. Model poisoning attacks, in which adversarially crafted data is inserted into the training pipeline, can corrupt predictions in ways that are difficult to detect through standard performance monitoring (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025a). The clinical stakes of an AI misdiagnosis make this a threat category warranting dedicated research attention.
These threat categories collectively reinforce the argument that security in 6G healthcare systems cannot be retrofitted, it must be embedded by design across every architectural layer, from physical radio interface to application logic (d. A. A. Al-Mekhlafi et al., 2024). Multi-layer security architectures combining physical-layer encryption, quantum-resistant key exchange protocols, next-generation authentication frameworks, and blockchain-based audit trails constitute the current state of proposed defensive infrastructure (H. 6.2 Real-time Data Transmission Safeguards
Protecting health data in transmission involves four interlocking properties, each addressing a distinct dimension of the security problem.
1.   Confidentiality: Health information must remain inaccessible to unauthorised parties throughout the entire data lifecycle; acquisition, transmission, storage, and retrieval. End-to-end encryption using algorithms robust against anticipated computational advances, including post-quantum cryptographic schemes, is the foundational mechanism for enforcing this property (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c).
2.   Integrity: An attacker who cannot decrypt a data stream may nonetheless corrupt it, introducing errors into vital-sign telemetry or imaging data that could cause diagnostic misclassifications. Cryptographic hash functions and digital signature schemes provide integrity verification mechanisms that detect such manipulation.
3.   Availability: Clinical systems depending on real-time data connectivity cannot tolerate arbitrary service interruptions. DoS attacks targeting a smart hospital’s communication infrastructure could delay critical alerts for cardiac events or medication dosing errors with potentially fatal consequences. Redundant communication pathways and automatic failover mechanisms are standard components of availability-focused design.
4.   Authenticity and Anonymity: Mutual authentication, the process by which both communicating parties verify the identity of the other prevents data injection from unauthorised devices masquerading as legitimate medical sensors. Anonymisation techniques that decouple identifiable patient attributes from transmitted health parameters protect privacy in contexts where data traverses shared network infrastructure (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c).
6.3 Compliance and Ethical AI Design
Regulatory Compliance
The regulatory landscape governing health data processing is both demanding and jurisdiction-specific. In the United States, HIPAA establishes minimum standards for protecting individually identifiable health information. In the European Union, GDPR imposes comparably stringent requirements with heightened penalty provisions for serious violations (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c). Healthcare organisations deploying 6G-IoMT infrastructure across borders must navigate both frameworks simultaneously, a compliance challenge requiring coordinated legal, technical, and organisational effort (Kaur & Gupta, 2025b).

Three of these principles warrant particular elaboration given their direct operational implications.
1.   Fairness and Bias Mitigation: Training datasets that underrepresent particular demographic groups produce models whose diagnostic accuracy varies systematically across those groups, effectively encoding existing health disparities into automated clinical tools. Addressing this requires deliberate curation of training data to ensure demographic breadth, ongoing algorithmic auditing, and the deployment of fairness-aware learning techniques (Kaur & Gupta, 2025b).
2.   Transparency and Explainability (XAI): A clinician who cannot understand the basis for an AI-generated recommendation cannot exercise meaningful professional oversight over it. In practice, opaque AI systems erode clinician confidence and impede adoption, even when those recommendations are objectively accurate (Kaur & Gupta, 2025b). Explainable AI techniques, including SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations), LIME (Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations), and DiCE (Diverse Counterfactual Explanations), address this limitation by producing human-interpretable accounts of AI reasoning (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025e).
3.   Accountability and Human Oversight: When an AI system contributes to a misdiagnosis or an inappropriate treatment recommendation, the question of who bears responsibility remains insufficiently resolved in most jurisdictions. Frameworks that specify accountability at each point in the decision chain, and that preserve meaningful human oversight as AI integration deepens, are a prerequisite for the trustworthy scaling of AI in clinical care (Lekadir et al., 2025).



7. CHALLENGES AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS
Even granting the technical promise of 6G-IoMT convergence, a number of persistent obstacles separate current pilot-scale achievements from full clinical deployment at population scale.
7.1 Technical Bottlenecks: Power, Spectrum, Interoperability
1.   Power Consumption: A network supporting billions of continuously transmitting medical sensors imposes substantial energy demands at both the device and infrastructure levels. Battery-constrained wearables that must sustain high-frequency data transmission without user intervention for charging face a fundamental tension between transmission power and operational longevity. Energy harvesting from ambient thermal, kinetic, and electromagnetic sources is under active investigation as a partial solution (MedicalExpo, 2024), alongside environmental intelligence algorithms that modulate transmission power dynamically in response to real-time channel conditions.
2.   Spectrum Utilisation: Sixth-generation networks will exploit a frequency range extending from sub-1 GHz through to beyond 100 GHz, including the terahertz band. Commercial exploitation at these frequencies requires regulatory processes; national allocation, international harmonisation, interference management, that are intrinsically slow relative to the pace of technology development. Proactive regulatory engagement beginning well before commercial deployment will be essential (How AI-Driven Diagnostics Are Transforming Healthcare Delivery-DelveInsight, 2025).
3.   Interoperability: The interoperability deficit is well-established in health informatics, but 6G deployment stands to amplify its consequences significantly. When device counts scale from thousands to millions within a single health system, incompatible data formats and communication protocols become commensurately more harmful (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025c). Universal communication standards, developed through inclusive multi-stakeholder processes and enforced through regulation or procurement requirements, are the necessary precondition for a genuinely unified IoMT ecosystem.
4.   Data Scarcity for AI Training: The accuracy and generalisability of AI diagnostic models depends heavily on the volume and representativeness of labelled training data. In many clinical specialties, high-quality labelled datasets of sufficient size simply do not exist; in others, they exist but are not shared across institutional boundaries for privacy or competitive reasons (Kaur & Gupta, 2025b). Federated learning approaches address this problem partially, but do not eliminate the underlying challenge of ensuring data diversity.
7.2 Research Trends: Novel Sensors, Federated AI, Edge Computing
Against these bottlenecks, a set of research directions is advancing simultaneously across multiple fronts.
1.   Novel Sensors: Wearable sensor technology continues to advance along multiple axes: miniaturisation, sensing breadth, energy efficiency, and biocompatibility. Research programmes are demonstrating sensors capable of monitoring breathing rate, sweat composition, and tear electrolyte profiles at clinically useful precision (S. S. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Hagri, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025a). Contactless sensing modalities, radar-based systems inferring vital signs without physical contact, offer monitoring for patients who cannot tolerate wearable devices, including infants and certain cognitively impaired populations.
2.   Federated AI (Federated Learning): Federated Learning (FL) resolves the central tension between the need for large, diverse training datasets and the regulatory constraints on centralising identifiable health records. FL distributes the training process across multiple nodes; individual hospitals, clinics, or patient devices, such that each node trains on its local data and contributes only model parameter updates to a global aggregation process (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025d). The resulting model benefits from the diversity of data held across all participating nodes without any single node’s patient data leaving its institutional perimeter.
3.   Edge Computing Optimisation: Research into edge computing for healthcare IoMT actively addresses the algorithmic and systems challenges of deploying AI inference at the network periphery. Intelligent task offloading frameworks that dynamically allocate computations between edge and cloud, energy-aware resource management schemes, and joint optimisation approaches balancing latency, throughput, and power consumption are all active areas (How AI-Driven Diagnostics Are Transforming Healthcare Delivery–DelveInsight, 2025).
4.   Digital Twins: The digital twin concept, a continuously updated virtual replica of a physical entity, is attracting significant research investment. Patient-level digital twins that integrate genomic, physiological, imaging, and behavioural data offer a simulation environment for personalised treatment planning without risk to the actual patient. Hospital-level twins enable operational optimisation and emergency preparedness modelling (Rajab, 2024).
5.   Explainable AI (XAI): The trajectory of XAI research in healthcare is moving from post-hoc explanation tools toward inherently interpretable architectures whose decision processes are transparent by design. Progress in this direction is expected to improve both clinical adoption of AI systems and their robustness to adversarial manipulation (A. Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi, Al-Mekhlafi and Al-Mekhlafi, 2025e).
These research directions are not independent. Federated learning addresses data scarcity while simultaneously preserving privacy. Edge computing reduces latency while confining sensitive data to its point of origin. XAI strengthens security by exposing model vulnerabilities while supporting the ethical requirement for clinical transparency. This convergence of technical solutions with ethical and regulatory imperatives suggests that the most effective research programmes in this domain are already beginning to reflect the interdisciplinary character of the challenge.
7.3 Strategic Policy and Cross-Disciplinary Collaboration
1.   Strategic Policy: The regulatory framework governing 6G spectrum allocation, security standards, data governance, and device certification is being constructed in real time, across jurisdictions with varying technical capacity and regulatory philosophies. Healthcare applications have requirements; particularly regarding reliability, security, and privacy, that must be explicitly accommodated in these frameworks rather than assumed to follow from general 6G standards. Security-by-design principles need to be incorporated into regulatory mandates at national and international levels from the outset.
2.   Cross-Disciplinary Collaboration: No single institution or sector commands all the expertise required to develop and deploy 6G-enabled healthcare systems at scale. Engineering competence in wireless communication and edge computing must combine with clinical knowledge, health informatics expertise, regulatory understanding, and social science insights into adoption barriers and equity implications. Collaborative initiatives such as Europe’s Hexa-X programme, Germany’s Open 6G Hub, and the United States’ Next G Alliance represent institutional attempts to organise this multi-disciplinary effort at the necessary scale.
3.   Equitable Access: The transformative potential of 6G-IoMT risks being concentrated in high-income health systems unless access and affordability are treated as explicit design objectives from the outset. Funding mechanisms that distribute infrastructure costs across public, private, and multilateral sources, and procurement frameworks that prioritise interoperability over proprietary lock-in, are essential conditions for equitable deployment.

8. CONCLUSION
The convergence of 6G wireless networks, IoMT devices, and AI-driven analytics constitutes one of the more consequential technological transitions currently underway in medicine. The significance of this convergence lies not in any single application; though remote surgery, continuous chronic disease monitoring, and personalised treatment optimisation are each transformative in their own right, but in the cumulative reorientation of healthcare from episodic, reactive management to continuous, anticipatory care.
Sixth-generation’s foundational properties; sub-microsecond latency, terabit-per-second throughput, AI-native network architecture, and ubiquitous coverage, provide the technical substrate for this reorientation. IoMT devices populate that substrate with physiological intelligence. AI systems extract clinically actionable insight from the resulting data streams. The synergy between these components is multiplicative, not additive: the capabilities they generate together substantially exceed what any one of them could accomplish alone.
Realising this potential, however, will require sustained attention to challenges that are as much institutional and regulatory as they are technical. The following conditions are necessary.
1.   Security by Design: Security architecture must be embedded at every layer of the system from the outset, not applied as post-deployment remediation. The threat categories associated with 6G’s distinctive capabilities (HRLLC attacks, AI model poisoning, ubiquitous breach propagation) demand proactive, specialised defensive frameworks.
2.   Universal Interoperability Standards: Enforced across the device and platform ecosystem rather than merely aspired to, interoperability standards are the necessary precondition for a unified IoMT architecture that actually functions at scale.
3.   Ethical AI Governance: Fairness, transparency, and human oversight must function as genuine design constraints, not compliance afterthoughts. XAI and federated learning are technical instruments in service of these ethical requirements.
4.   Equitable Distribution: The geographic and socioeconomic distribution of 6G-IoMT benefits requires deliberate policy choices. Technical feasibility does not guarantee equitable access; that requires coordinated investment and regulatory action.
When these conditions are met, the healthcare revolution that 6G-IoMT convergence makes technically possible can become a practical and equitable reality. The next decade represents the critical window in which those foundational decisions must be made.
