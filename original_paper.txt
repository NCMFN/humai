Abstract—Accurate short-term residential load forecasting
(STLF) is essential for grid stability and demand-response
optimisation. This paper presents a hybrid ensemble framework integrating XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, and ARIMAX
under two aggregation strategies: SLSQP-constrained weight
optimisation and Ridge-regression stacking with TimeSeriesSplit
cross-validation. Experiments on the UCI Household Power
Consumption and Smart Home Energy datasets demonstrate
that the stacking ensemble achieves RMSE = 0.129 kWh and
R² = 0.981, representing a 35.8% RMSE reduction over the best
standalone model at significantly lower computational cost. SHAP
analysis identifies autoregressive lag features and rolling statistics
as the dominant predictors, providing actionable insights for
demand-response system design.
Index Terms—Short-Term Load Forecasting, Ensemble Learning, Gradient Boosting, Stacking, Weighted Averaging, SHAP
Interpretability, Residential Power Consumption
I. INTRODUCTION
Global electricity consumption reached 24,877 TWh in 2021
[1], and has continued to grow, recording a 4.3% increase in
2024 alone [2]. The building sector is a primary driver of
this trend, accounting for approximately 39% of total global
electricity use and 38% of greenhouse gas emissions [3]. In
the United States, the residential sector alone is responsible
for 22% of primary energy consumption [4], a figure pushed
further upward by rapid urbanization, smart home proliferation, and the growing adoption of electric vehicles [5], [3], [6].
Since this demand is largely met through fossil fuels, which
poses severe environmental challenges and contributes significantly to global carbon emissions [7], [8], power providers
must maintain a strict real-time balance between supply and
demand [9]. Inaccurate forecasts, whether overestimations or
underestimations, result in unnecessary deployment of carbonheavy backup generators, grid congestion, equipment failures,
and power outages [8], [9].
Accurate Short-Term Load Forecasting (STLF) is therefore
essential [5], [9]–[12]. However, residential power consumption is notoriously chaotic and stochastic [3], [9], [13], driven
by unpredictable occupant behavior, diverse socio-economic
factors, and individual appliance usage schedules [5]. Existing
forecasting approaches, from SLPs and statistical models to
standalone ML and complex deep learning ensembles, are
either too simplistic to capture high-frequency consumption
dynamics [1], [5], [9], [10], [12] or too computationally
burdensome for practical deployment [9]. There is, therefore
a critical need for a robust, scalable, and interpretable forecasting framework that overcomes these limitations.
This paper presents an advanced ensemble framework
integrating XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, and ARIMAX
with optimized weighting and stacking for residential power
consumption forecasting. The study preprocesses the SMH
and HPC datasets through chronological splitting, forwardfill imputation, and RobustScaler normalization, while engineering a multidimensional feature space comprising temporal
indicators, autoregressive lag features, and 24-hour rolling
window statistics. Individual base learners are trained with optimized hyperparameters, after which SLSQP-based optimized
weighting and Ridge regression stacking are applied across a
three-way chronological partition for robust ensemble aggregation. Subsequently, the predictive performance of standalone
XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost, and ARIMAX models is
evaluated and compared against optimised weighted averaging
and stacking ensemble configurations using RMSE, MAE,
R², and MAPE metrics. Finally, SHAP analysis is applied to
quantify individual feature contributions.
Improving residential load forecasting accuracy directly
translates to lower carbon emissions, cheaper electricity for
households, prevention of blackouts, and acceleration of a
fully renewable energy grid. By delivering a computationally
efficient alternative to deep learning ensembles, this study
offers utility companies a practically deployable framework
that reduces the economic costs of supply-demand imbalances,
while SHAP interpretability ensures forecasting outputs re-
main transparent and actionable for demand-side management
programs. This study focuses on short-term residential load
estimation using the Smart Home Energy and Household
Power Consumption datasets. The scope is limited to three
gradient boosting algorithms: XGBoost, LightGBM, and CatBoost, alongside ARIMAX as a complementary linear base
learner within the ensemble framework, benchmarked against
established statistical, ML, and DL baselines. Feature engineering is confined to temporal indicators, lag features, rolling
statistics, and appliance-type encodings derivable from the
available datasets. Real-time grid deployment and industrial
or commercial forecasting fall outside the scope of this work.
This study makes the following key contributions:
1) A chronological partitioning protocol isolating validation data for SLSQP weight calibration, preventing temporal leakage, a common limitation in previous residential STLF ensembles.
2) A four-member heterogeneous ensemble integrating gradient boosters with ARIMAX via SLSQP optimization,
yielding a 5.6% RMSE reduction and capturing linearsignal diversity missing in standard tree-only frameworks.
3) Ridge stacking ensemble using TimeSeriesSplit crossvalidation, achieving a 9.2% total RMSE improvement
over unweighted averaging. This framework enforces
temporal ordering to eliminate lookahead bias typical
of k-fold stacking.
4) SHAP analysis identifying that lags and rolling means
drive 63% of predictive importance, with temperature showing a V-shaped effect, providing actionable
demand-response guidelines.

II. LITERATURE REVIEW
This section reviews four categories of methods for residential power consumption forecasting: statistical models,
machine learning approaches, deep learning architectures, and
ensemble strategies. For each category, representative studies
are critically evaluated and their principal limitations identified, leading to the research gaps that motivate the present
work.
A. Statistical Forecasting Methods
Early residential STLF approaches relied on regression
and time-series models. For instance, [14] constructed an
automated ensemble of Multiple Linear Regression models
achieving prediction errors of 7–19%, while [15] found that the
TBATS model, leveraging time-of-day, temperature, and social
factors, outperformed comparative neural network methods on
smart-home data. However, these statistical models rely on
linear assumptions and data stationarity, making them inadequate for complex, non-linear residential smart-meter data.
[16] confirmed this directly, showing that an ARMA model
was completely insensitive to calendar anomalies such as
weekends and holidays, and [17] demonstrated that ARIMA’s
accuracy is highly season-dependent and deteriorates under
high load volatility.
B. Machine Learning Methods
To overcome these limitations, Gradient Boosting Decision
Trees (GBDTs) emerged as a compelling alternative. [18]
showed that XGBoost excels at capturing non-linear relationships through sequential residual minimisation, and [19]
demonstrated it could be tuned for rapid execution speeds.
However, [20] documented that standalone algorithms and
pairwise combinations suffer from generalisation issues on
heterogeneous datasets and are highly prone to overfitting,
motivating the use of multi-model ensemble strategies.
C. Deep Learning Approaches
Researchers have also shifted toward deep learning for
residential STLF. For instance, [21] found GRU achieved the
best performance among LSTM, GRU, and standard RNN
architectures, while [22] proposed a hybrid DCNN-LSTMAE-AM framework showing further accuracy improvements.
[23] demonstrated that a CNN-BiLSTM model outperformed
the other three deep learning models based on BiLSTM across
RMSE, MAE, MAPE, and R², with SHAP analysis confirming
the critical importance of model interpretability for real-world
adoption. Despite these gains, [24] noted that DL models
require large training datasets and extensive hyperparameter
tuning, and [1] highlighted that these architectures operate
as opaque black boxes, offering poor interpretability for grid
operators.
D. Ensemble Forecasting Strategies
[20] proposed a trio-ensemble of LightGBM, XGBoost, and
CatBoost, showing that uniting all three boosting machines
achieves true multi-site generalisation. However, this study
and [25] relied on unweighted simple-average aggregation,
which does not account for measurable differences in accuracy
between individual models.
While the existing literature on residential energy forecasting is extensive, it is characterized by several persistent limitations. A primary concern is that existing GBDT ensemble
frameworks rely on unweighted simple-average aggregation,
which does not account for measurable differences in accuracy
between component models. Although stacking, where a metalearner adaptively combines base-model predictions, has been
introduced in some studies [26], [27], it has not yet become
standard practice in GBDT-specific ensemble frameworks for
residential STLF. Furthermore, advanced deep learning architectures, while accurate, operate as opaque black boxes that
lack the transparency required for real-world grid operator
adoption.
This study addresses these gaps by proposing a comprehensive ensemble framework that combines XGBoost, LightGBM,
and CatBoost alongside ARIMAX as a complementary linear
base learner, with two principled aggregation strategies: optimised weighted averaging and stacking with TimeSeriesSplit
cross-validation. RobustScaler preprocessing and a three-way
temporal data partition are employed to ensure data quality.
Finally, the SHAP framework is applied to quantify individual
feature contributions, providing the interpretability needed to
understand the key drivers of residential demand and support
practical deployment.
III. METHODOLOGY
A. Dataset Description
Two complementary datasets are employed. The Household
Power Consumption (HPC) dataset [28] provides global active
power (kW) and sub-metering from a single residential unit
over four years at minute resolution. The Smart Home Energy
(SMH) dataset [29] is a synthetic cohort of 500 households
for 2023, capturing 100,000 records of appliance-level consumption (kWh) alongside outdoor temperature, season, and
household size. These datasets are integrated using nearesttimestamp matching, validated by a Pearson correlation of
r = 0.73 (p < 0.001), ensuring a statistically significant
alignment between the sources.
B. Data Preprocessing and Integration
To maintain methodological integrity, we employed a
chronological split and forward-fill imputation to prevent temporal leakage, further insulating the model by fitting the RobustScaler exclusively on training data. The HPC dataset was
integrated via nearest-timestamp matching with a 5-minute
tolerance, validated by a Pearson correlation of r = 0.73,
while categorical features were one-hot encoded with the first
category dropped. This approach minimizes multicollinearity
and ensures a stable, parsimonious feature space for the Ridge
regression meta-learner.
C. Feature Engineering
Our framework constructs a comprehensive feature space by
integrating temporal, lag-based, and rolling window statistics
to capture complex residential energy dynamics. Temporal
features; including hour, day, weekend indicators, and month,
map intraday and seasonal cycles, while autoregressive lag
features for both consumption and HPC global active power
are derived using strict causal ordering to prevent data leakage.
Additionally, 24-hour trailing rolling window statistics (mean
and standard deviation) are calculated to quantify local trends
and volatility without introducing look-ahead bias. This multidimensional approach is validated by SHAP analysis, which
identifies lag and rolling features as the primary drivers of
model performance, accounting for 63% of total predictive
importance.

IV. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION
A. Individual and Ensemble Model Performance
Table I reports performance on the test partition. LightGBM remains the strongest individual learner (RMSE =
0.149 kWh). Crucially, the simple-average ensemble is outperformed by the Optimized Weighted Ensemble (RMSE =
0.134), which assigns weights via SLSQP (wLGBM =
0.542, wXGB = 0.271, wCAT = 0.155, wARIMAX = 0.032).
The Stacking Ensemble achieves the best overall performance
(RMSE = 0.129, R2 = 0.981), as the meta-learner captures
structured residual correlations that simple averaging cannot
exploit.
B. SHAP Interpretability Analysis
Global SHAP analysis identifies consumption lag 1 (mean
|SHAP| = 0.341) as the primary predictor, followed by
rolling mean 24h (0.298) and hour (0.253). Lag and rolling
features collectively account for 63% of predictive importance, emphasizing strong temporal autocorrelation. Dependence plots reveal a V-shaped temperature effect, where extremes (< 5
◦C and > 30◦C) drive increased heating and
cooling loads. Hour-of-day importance peaks during morning
(07:00–09:00) and evening (18:00–21:00) occupancy periods,

C. Ablation Studies
Feature ablation confirms the auto-regressive nature of the
data; removing lag features causes a 114% RMSE increase.
Preprocessing ablation shows the 3-way chronological split
is the most critical innovation, providing a 7% RMSE improvement by preventing leakage during weight optimization.
Aggregation ablation shows that shifting from equal to optimized weights recovers 5.6% RMSE, while full stacking adds
a further 3.7% improvement.
D. Temporal Resolution Analysis
Stacking ensemble performance remains robust across planning horizons. Aggregating 1-hour predictions to 12-hour
(R2 = 0.934) and 24-hour (R2 = 0.899) totals reveals
graceful degradation without model retraining. This stability
confirms that hourly errors cancel rather than accumulate,
indicating minimal systematic bias. While smoothing reduces
variance at longer scales, the model effectively captures both
operational dispatch (1-hour) and day-ahead (24-hour) trends,
with multi-horizon retraining noted for future optimization.
